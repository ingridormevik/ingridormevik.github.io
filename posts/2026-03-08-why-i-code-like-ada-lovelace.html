Why I code the way I do

Why I Code Like Ada Lovelace

Not as a performance of purity. Not as a fantasy of lone genius. As feminist method. As remix. As survival. As a way to turn code into atmosphere, community memory, queer storytelling, and work that actually moves.

She wanted time with her children. I want time on mountains, time with my community, time to build immersive worlds, and time to stay fully alive inside the life I am actually living.
Women’s Day mood
Female coding empowerment is not about pretending the work is easy.

It is about refusing the lie that only one type of builder counts. Women, girls, and femmes build with fragments, intuition, rigor, aesthetics, adaptation, unfinished certainty, and vision. That still counts. It counts completely.

Current practice

HTML appropriated. CSS transformed. JavaScript remixed. Sound-reactive interfaces. Regnbuenatt worldbuilding. Queer digital atmosphere. Strategic community design.

I chose Ada Lovelace and Alan Turing for my computing history exam. Hours of research led me here: Ada did not write her Notes to stage some timeless myth of genius. She wrote because repetitive calculation was tedious, because intellect mattered, because life was already full, and because automation could create room for more meaningful thought. That lands differently when read through Women’s Day. It lands personally.

The Process

I copy code. Ask AI to extract colors. Generate gradients. Tweak spacing. Rewrite tone. Rebuild sections. Add motion. Test on mobile. Make it more immersive. Make it more editorial. Make it clearer. Ship it.

If you are waiting for an apology, you are still reading the wrong page.

What Ada Actually Wanted

In 1840, while trying to work on Babbage’s Analytical Engine with two toddlers and a baby, Ada wrote with a startling honesty that still cuts: “How many, many moments there are, when their presence is most irritating and intolerable to me.”

She called her Notes “my first-born” while she had three actual children under eight. Her life was not a clean narrative of genius unfolding in a vacuum. It was crowded, demanding, embodied, interrupted, and intellectually alive. That complexity matters to me more than hero worship ever could.

My Version

I work in digital culture, queer storytelling, community-facing design, and social media strategy. I build things that need to feel alive, not just function. I study. I research. I organize. I make work for real people and real communities, not for the fantasy of an empty room where one perfect coder invents everything alone.

If I spent all my cognitive labor writing every single line of CSS from nothing, I would have less left for accessibility, concept, narrative atmosphere, research, editing, ethical framing, queer community context, and the actual reason the work exists.

AI extracts palettes. AI helps restructure. AI helps debug. I focus on intent: what does this page need to feel like, what should this interface communicate, who is it for, what kind of world does it build, and what politics live inside its aesthetics.

Posthuman Work

Katherine Hayles writes about informational patterns moving across bodies and machines. That feels close to how I work in practice. I collect code fragments, color systems, references, prompts, prose, interface logic, emotional textures, and design intentions, then move them between tools until something coherent emerges.

My webpage is not the product of isolated genius. It is distributed agency. My design thinking plus existing code plus AI processing plus queer community needs plus revision plus feedback becomes a page that works. That is not lesser. It is simply more honest.

Byron Russell’s writing on appropriation and remix matters here too. Transformation is not the absence of authorship. It is often how authorship actually happens in digital culture.

Women Who Build Are Not Required to Perform Purity

One of the most exhausting myths in tech is that legitimacy comes from visible suffering. As if the only respectable builder is the one who writes everything from nothing and never accepts assistance. As if elegance only counts when it begins in isolation and ends in exhaustion.

I reject that. Female coding empowerment means using every available tool with intention, intelligence, style, and purpose. It means adaptation. It means synthesis. It means refusing shame around collaboration, iteration, reuse, and machine assistance.

Women’s Day test:

Is she still a real coder if she remixes, directs, transforms, art-directs, and ships instead of worshipping difficulty?

Answer: yes.

What matters is whether the work carries thought, reaches people, holds feeling, and makes something possible for others.

Time Outside vs Time Writing Hex Code

I would still rather spend three hours hiking than three hours manually calculating every visual choice from scratch. Not because coding lacks value. Because my life needs range. My body needs motion. My creativity needs air. My community work needs energy.

Ada wanted time with her children. I want time with mountains, with activism, with my people, with my nervous system, and with the parts of myself that are not productive but still essential. I am not interested in wasting life proving I can memorize syntax as a ritual of worthiness.

Code as Commons

Code is collective knowledge. Design patterns are shared culture. Stack Overflow snippets, GitHub fragments, tutorials, remixed JavaScript, AI-assisted restructuring, and visual references are part of how digital culture actually works: iterative, collaborative, cumulative, and built on what others made available.

The myth of the lone coder serves branding and gatekeeping far more than it serves actual creative practice.

What I’m Actually Good At

Good at: atmosphere, concept, accessibility, digital storytelling, emotional tone, editorial composition, cultural context, queer worldbuilding, and knowing when an interface has a soul.

Decent at: finding solutions, directing AI, adapting code, building from fragments, and iterating fast.

Still learning: elegant architecture, memorized syntax, and lower-level implementation details.

That does not weaken the work. It clarifies how the work is made.

The Point

Efficiency is not laziness. It is the strategic redistribution of cognitive labor toward human priorities.

Ada wrote algorithms so she could live beyond calculation. I use AI collaboration and remix-based coding so I can study, organize, research, build Regnbuenatt, experiment with sound-reactive interfaces, make community-facing work, and still have enough self left to remain human.

The wheel exists. So do shared libraries, snippets, prompts, and machine assistance. I am not here to prove I invented the wheel. I am here to build something luminous with it.

Final thought:

Ada did not need purity politics. Neither do I.
